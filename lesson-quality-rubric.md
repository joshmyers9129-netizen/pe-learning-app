# Lesson Quality Rubric
## PE Learning App — Private Markets Fluency for CFA Charterholders

---

## Overview

This rubric governs quality standards for all lesson content in this private-markets learning app. The target learner is a **CFA charterholder** with strong public-markets intuition — fluent in DCF, WACC, factor models, fixed income mechanics, financial accounting, and equity valuation — but with limited practical fluency in private equity mechanics, fund economics, or allocator-level PE reasoning. The learner's explicit goals are to operate confidently in PE manager meetings, understand GP behavior and incentives, and build allocator-level judgment for portfolio construction and OCIO-relevant decision-making.

**This is a premium standard.** Lessons are not blog posts or MOOC slides. They are structured instructional artifacts intended to produce durable, applicable knowledge — the kind that holds up in a real diligence meeting or a real portfolio review. Every lesson must earn its stated time claim, and every minute of a learner's attention must pay off.

---

## Lesson Types

### Type 1 · Standard Lesson

**Target duration:** 15–25 minutes
**Word count guidance:** 1,800–2,800 words of body content (adjusted upward ~200 words per load-bearing visual the learner must study)

**Purpose:** Build a clear mental model around one core PE concept. Introduce PE-specific terminology, mechanics, or frameworks with enough depth to prevent false fluency. Bridge from public-markets intuition where relevant; flag where that intuition breaks.

**Block structure:**
- 1 intro block — relevance framing, why this matters for meetings or allocator work
- 2–3 teaching blocks — concept, mechanism, nuance
- 1 example block — concrete, realistic, fund-economics-calibrated
- 1 exercise block — applied, short, judgment-demanding
- Quiz: 2–3 multiple-choice questions + 1 short-response prompt
- Visuals: 0–1 load-bearing instructional visual
- Sources: cited to recognizable authority (ILPA, CAIA, CFA Institute, Preqin)

**Appropriate topics:** Fund structure and closed-end mechanics, J-curve concept, IRR vs. MOIC tradeoffs, LP/GP economics, capital call and distribution mechanics, basic fee structures (management fee, carry, preferred return), vintage year risk, blind-pool risk.

---

### Type 2 · Dense Technical Lesson

**Target duration:** 25–35 minutes
**Word count guidance:** 2,800–4,000 words of body content, accounting for worked examples and numerical exercises

**Purpose:** Deliver quantitative depth and precise mechanics for concepts where a rough understanding is worse than no understanding — where imprecision will visibly mislead in a real meeting or calculation. A learner who leaves with a hand-wavy sense of IRR timing sensitivity, waterfall construction, or PME methodology will perform worse than one who was never taught it.

**Block structure:**
- 1 intro block — why precision matters here; what breaks if you get this wrong
- 3–4 teaching blocks — concept, full mechanics, worked numerical example, edge cases
- 1–2 example blocks — at least one worked numeric at realistic fund economics, one contextual application
- 1 exercise block — numerical or structured multi-step analysis
- Quiz: 3–4 multiple-choice questions (at least one requiring computation or multi-step reasoning) + 1 short-response prompt
- Visuals: 1–2 load-bearing instructional visuals; at least one must carry content not efficiently expressed in prose
- Sources: specific citations preferred (ILPA Principles 3.0, CAIA Level II chapter and section, named academic paper, identified fund document type)

**Appropriate topics:** IRR calculation mechanics and timing sensitivity, distribution waterfall construction (American vs. European), TVPI/DPI/RVPI/PME relationships, management fee offset mechanics, LPA clawback provisions, pacing model math, subscription line credit facility impact on IRR, co-investment economics.

---

### Type 3 · Capstone / Checkpoint

**Target duration:** 20–30 minutes
**Word count guidance:** 2,000–3,200 words; emphasis on synthesis prompts and scenario analysis over new explanatory text

**Purpose:** Synthesize multiple prior concepts into integrated judgment. Simulate decision-making contexts: manager meeting analysis, LP diligence scenario, portfolio construction review, or risk assessment. More than half the lesson should be synthesis and application — this is not the place to introduce significant new content. The measure of success is whether a learner leaves reasoning differently, not merely knowing more.

**Block structure:**
- 1 intro block — integration framing; which prior concepts this checkpoint activates
- 1–2 synthesis blocks — connecting prior concepts with a new angle, application, or point of tension between concepts
- 1 case or scenario block — a realistic manager meeting excerpt, LP diligence decision, or portfolio construction prompt; long enough to require genuine analysis
- 1 judgment question or decision prompt — open-ended enough that a correct answer requires weighing trade-offs
- Quiz: 3–5 multiple-choice questions (emphasis on application and judgment, not recall) + 1 structured short-response prompt that requires reasoning through a scenario
- Visuals: 1–2 synthesis-oriented visuals (comparison tables, decision frameworks, annotated timelines)
- Sources: may reference prior lessons as integration context; external sources where new claims are introduced

**Appropriate contexts:** Day 10 review of PE fund economics and incentive alignment, manager meeting fluency simulation, pacing and portfolio construction decisions, comparing risk profiles across buyout vs. growth vs. venture, GP-led secondary vs. LP-led secondary decision analysis, OCIO-level manager selection signal review.

## Scoring Dimensions

All lessons are scored on an integer scale of **1–5** on each of the eleven dimensions below.

- Scores of **1–2** require a revision pass before publication.
- Score of **3** is the minimum acceptable floor for a standard lesson on most dimensions.
- Score of **4** is the minimum for dense technical and capstone lessons on most dimensions.
- See the [Minimum Score Summary](#minimum-score-summary) table for type-specific thresholds.

---

### Dimension 1 · Explanatory Depth

*Does the lesson go beyond definition? Does it cover the mechanism, the why, and at least one meaningful nuance or edge case?*

| Score | Descriptor |
|-------|------------|
| 5 | Covers mechanism, motivation, edge cases, and limits of the concept. Learner understands not just what, but why and when it breaks or misleads. Conceptual model is stress-tested. |
| 4 | Covers mechanism and at least one meaningful nuance or complication. Learner is not left with a simplified model that will mislead in a real meeting or calculation. |
| 3 | Covers mechanism clearly but omits edge cases and nuance. Sufficient for foundational concepts with low real-world complexity; insufficient for anything that will arise frequently in manager meetings or diligence. |
| 2 | Covers definition and surface description. No mechanism. A reasonably good Wikipedia summary. |
| 1 | Surface definition only. Names the concept without explaining it. |

**Minimum:** Standard ≥ 3 · Dense Technical ≥ 4 · Capstone ≥ 3 *(depth covered in prior lessons; synthesis emphasized here)*

---

### Dimension 2 · Conceptual Clarity

*Is the central mental model clean and unambiguous? Does the lesson prevent false fluency by flagging where public-markets intuition misleads in PE contexts?*

| Score | Descriptor |
|-------|------------|
| 5 | The concept is organized around one clean, memorable mental model. Where public-markets analogies break — e.g., where IRR, duration, or leverage mean something materially different in PE — the break is named explicitly. False fluency is actively prevented. |
| 4 | The mental model is clear and well-organized. At least one common misconception is named and corrected. |
| 3 | The mental model is clear but misconceptions are not addressed. Learner is not confused but is also not protected from prior confusion they arrived with. |
| 2 | Multiple framings are introduced without hierarchy or resolution. Learner is left uncertain which mental model governs. |
| 1 | Unclear or contradictory framing. Learner may be more confused at exit than at entry. |

**Minimum:** Standard ≥ 3 · Dense Technical ≥ 4 · Capstone ≥ 4

---

### Dimension 3 · Manager-Meeting Transfer

*Would a person who completed this lesson be meaningfully better prepared to sit in a PE manager meeting — able to follow the manager's reasoning, ask one level deeper, or recognize a red flag they would previously have missed?*

| Score | Descriptor |
|-------|------------|
| 5 | The lesson directly simulates or anticipates manager-meeting language, framing, or diligence logic. Learner leaves with specific vocabulary, follow-up question frames, or red-flag awareness that would be visible — and useful — in a real manager meeting. |
| 4 | The lesson equips the learner to follow manager language on this topic and ask one level deeper. The meeting-relevance connection is made explicit, not left to inference. |
| 3 | The lesson builds relevant knowledge but the connection to meetings is not made explicit. Transfer is assumed rather than designed. A motivated learner can bridge it; a distracted one will not. |
| 2 | Conceptual or theoretical. Learner understands the concept academically but has no bridge to how it surfaces in conversation, a tear sheet, or a manager presentation. |
| 1 | No practical dimension. Could be a textbook entry. Reading it would not change behavior in a meeting. |

**Minimum:** Standard ≥ 3 · Dense Technical ≥ 3 · Capstone ≥ 4

---

### Dimension 4 · Allocator / OCIO Transfer

*Does the lesson build decision-useful skill for portfolio construction, pacing, manager selection, or OCIO-level oversight? Does it help the learner think as a capital allocator — someone responsible for PE portfolio results — not merely as a student of PE mechanics?*

| Score | Descriptor |
|-------|------------|
| 5 | The lesson explicitly develops allocator reasoning: sizing, pacing, vintage diversification, manager selection signal, risk-adjusted return framing, or portfolio-level consequence. A learner could draw on this in an actual portfolio review or manager selection decision. |
| 4 | Clear allocator relevance with at least one explicit connection to a real portfolio decision, oversight consideration, or manager selection criterion. |
| 3 | Allocator relevance is implicit — a motivated reader can construct it — but not surfaced by the lesson itself. |
| 2 | Primarily fund-level or deal-level mechanics with no bridge to the allocator perspective. |
| 1 | No allocator dimension. Fund mechanics presented in isolation, with no signal about why an LP would care or what it changes in portfolio construction. |

**Minimum:** Standard ≥ 2 · Dense Technical ≥ 3 · Capstone ≥ 4

---

### Dimension 5 · Realism and Richness of Examples

*Are examples drawn from plausible fund economics, realistic deal structures, or recognizable market conditions? Do they use real-order-of-magnitude numbers that build accurate intuition?*

| Score | Descriptor |
|-------|------------|
| 5 | Examples use fund-realistic economics: $500M–$5B buyout fund range, 2%/20% structure as baseline with named variants, 6–8% preferred return, 5–7 year investment period, 10-year fund term with extension. Numbers are internally consistent and economically valid. Deal types and dynamics are recognizable to a practitioner. |
| 4 | Examples are realistic and internally consistent. Numbers are plausible. Minor simplifications present but not misleading. |
| 3 | Examples are correct but simplified or generic ("a fund invests $100 in a company"). Numbers may be pedagogically clean but economically implausible at real fund scale. Learner gets the logic but builds wrong intuition about real-world magnitudes. |
| 2 | Examples are invented in ways that produce wrong intuition about PE economics — implausible returns, implausible fund sizes, implausible structures. |
| 1 | No meaningful examples, or examples are placeholder-only ("consider a fund that..."). |

**Calibration anchors:** Top-quartile buyout net TVPI historically ~2.0–2.5x; median net IRR 12–16%; typical commitment size for institutional LP 1–5% of PE allocation per fund; fund life with extensions commonly 12–14 years.

**Minimum:** Standard ≥ 3 · Dense Technical ≥ 4 · Capstone ≥ 4

---

### Dimension 6 · Instructional Value of Visuals

*Do visuals teach the concept, or are they decoration? Is each visual load-bearing — meaning the learner would lose meaningful understanding by its absence?*

| Score | Descriptor |
|-------|------------|
| 5 | Every visual is load-bearing. Each communicates something the surrounding text cannot do as efficiently. Visuals are accurately labeled, internally consistent with surrounding content, and explicitly referenced in prose. |
| 4 | Visuals are primarily load-bearing. At least one materially accelerates comprehension — a learner would struggle to build the mental model without it. Labels and axes are present where applicable. |
| 3 | Visuals are present and correct but primarily illustrative. Could be removed without significant comprehension loss. Decorative-leaning but not harmful or misleading. |
| 2 | Visuals are misleading, unlabeled, or contradict surrounding content. The lesson would be better without them. |
| 1 | No visuals where a visual would materially help, or visuals actively introduce confusion. |

**Scoring note for visual-free lessons:** A lesson with no visuals is scored on whether a visual was warranted. A standard lesson covering a purely verbal concept (e.g., fiduciary obligations, ILPA principles framing) may score 4 with zero visuals if no visual would improve comprehension. A lesson covering J-curve mechanics, waterfall distributions, fund cashflow timing, or IRR sensitivity that has no visual scores 2 or below regardless of prose quality.

**Minimum:** Standard ≥ 3 · Dense Technical ≥ 4 · Capstone ≥ 3

---

### Dimension 7 · Source Grounding

*Are factual claims, standard terms, and performance assertions traceable to identifiable authoritative sources? Are sources cited in a way a motivated learner could follow and verify?*

| Score | Descriptor |
|-------|------------|
| 5 | Substantive claims are grounded in specific, verifiable sources: ILPA Principles 3.0, CAIA Level II Chapter and section, a named SEC guidance document, a specific Preqin data release, a named academic paper. A learner who distrusts a claim can find and check it. |
| 4 | Major claims are grounded. Sources are named and recognizable (ILPA, CAIA curriculum, CFA Institute private markets materials, Preqin, Cambridge Associates benchmark methodology). Generic citations are acceptable for foundational material if supplemented by at least one specific reference for novel or contested claims. |
| 3 | Sources are listed but generic: "CFA Institute private markets materials," "CAIA curriculum." Verifiable in principle but not specific. Acceptable for broadly settled foundational content; insufficient for technical details, contested conventions, or performance statistics. |
| 2 | Sources are vague, absent for important claims, or aspirationally cited (e.g., "industry standard" without a reference). Claims rest on the authority of the app alone. |
| 1 | No sources. All claims are bare assertions. |

**Minimum:** Standard ≥ 3 · Dense Technical ≥ 4 · Capstone ≥ 3

---

### Dimension 8 · Retention / Retrieval Support

*Does the lesson structure actively support long-term retention? Does the quiz test genuine recall and application, or merely reading comprehension and pattern matching?*

| Score | Descriptor |
|-------|------------|
| 5 | Quiz requires retrieval from memory rather than re-reading. At least one question requires multi-step application or judgment, not recognition. Short-response prompt demands synthesis or a trade-off analysis that a learner who partially understood the lesson would answer differently than one who fully understood it. Distractors in MC questions reflect real misconceptions — someone who half-learned the concept would choose a wrong answer confidently. |
| 4 | Quiz tests recall and at least one application. Distractors are not obviously wrong to an imprecise thinker. Short-response is open-ended enough to surface understanding gaps. |
| 3 | Quiz is correct and relevant but primarily tests reading comprehension or single-step recall. A learner who re-reads the relevant block immediately before the quiz scores identically to one who has retained it. Distractors are weak. Short-response prompt is under-constrained. |
| 2 | Quiz is formulaic. Correct answer is discoverable by sentence-structure parsing or obvious elimination of implausible distractors. Short response is a rephrasing exercise. |
| 1 | Quiz is absent, trivially easy, or not aligned with lesson content. |

**Minimum:** Standard ≥ 3 · Dense Technical ≥ 4 · Capstone ≥ 4

---

### Dimension 9 · Judgment-Building Value

*Does the lesson help the learner develop professional judgment — the ability to reason soundly through a novel situation — rather than merely accumulate vocabulary and facts?*

| Score | Descriptor |
|-------|------------|
| 5 | The lesson explicitly develops judgment: surfacing trade-offs, asking when the standard analysis breaks, showing how practitioners reason through ambiguous situations, or building a framework the learner can deploy in situations not covered by the lesson. A sophisticated reader finishes with sharpened intuition and a new decision lens, not merely new vocabulary. |
| 4 | Judgment is developed indirectly through one strong example, exercise, or framing question that forces the learner to weigh competing considerations, not just recall a correct answer. |
| 3 | Factual and applicable, but judgment is not a design goal. Good for building fluency and accurate recall; limited for independent reasoning in novel situations. |
| 2 | Purely informational. No application, no trade-off, no ambiguity engaged. Could be replaced by a high-quality glossary entry. |
| 1 | Factually accurate but builds no reasoning capacity. A reference card, not a lesson. |

**Minimum:** Standard ≥ 3 · Dense Technical ≥ 3 · Capstone ≥ 5

---

### Dimension 10 · Honest Duration Credibility

*Is the stated reading time honest? Would a motivated CFA-level reader finish the lesson — actively engaging with examples, exercises, and quiz — in the stated time, without rushing or with significant time remaining?*

| Score | Descriptor |
|-------|------------|
| 5 | Duration claim is accurate within ±3 minutes for a CFA-level reader engaging actively. Content density is correctly calibrated to the lesson type (standard vs. dense technical). |
| 4 | Duration claim is honest. May be off by 4–5 minutes in either direction but does not significantly misrepresent the time commitment. |
| 3 | Duration claim is moderately understated or overstated — a 20-minute claim takes 27–28 minutes, or a 25-minute claim takes 18 — but not enough to damage trust noticeably on a single lesson. Repeated misses across the curriculum compound into a credibility problem. |
| 2 | Duration claim is significantly off. A "15-minute" lesson with 4,000 words and two worked numeric examples, or a "30-minute" lesson a CFA reader completes comfortably in 10. |
| 1 | Duration appears to be a default value or guess with no calibration to actual content volume or complexity. |

**Calibration baseline:** CFA-level professional reading pace on structured technical content: approximately 220–260 words per minute with full comprehension, plus 2–3 minutes per load-bearing visual that requires analysis, plus 3–5 minutes for exercise and quiz engagement. A 20-minute standard lesson budget supports roughly 2,400–2,800 words of prose plus one visual and a 3-question quiz.

**Minimum:** Standard ≥ 4 · Dense Technical ≥ 4 · Capstone ≥ 4

---

### Dimension 11 · Appropriateness for a CFA / Public-Markets Learner

*Is the lesson precisely calibrated to a CFA charterholder with strong public-equity fluency? Does it build on what the learner already knows rather than reteaching it? Does it bridge deliberately from public-markets intuition and flag where that intuition breaks?*

| Score | Descriptor |
|-------|------------|
| 5 | The lesson is calibrated precisely to the target learner. It assumes full competence with DCF, WACC, factor models, financial accounting, portfolio theory, fixed income mechanics, and the analytical rigor of CFA-level thinking — without reteaching any of it. It uses public-markets analogies deliberately and explicitly marks where they stop working. PE-native concepts are introduced at professional pitch, not introductory pitch. |
| 4 | Well-calibrated. Occasional light over-explanation of public-markets concepts, but not patronizing. PE concepts are introduced at appropriate depth for the learner. |
| 3 | Accessible but imprecisely calibrated. Reads more like generic financial education than PE-specific instruction for a sophisticated learner. A CFA charterholder would find it correct but under-pitched. |
| 2 | Miscalibrated in one direction: either over-explains public-markets basics the learner mastered years ago (condescending and time-wasting), or under-explains PE mechanics with no bridge from public-markets intuition (unhelpful and disorienting). |
| 1 | Miscalibrated in both directions, or written for a general financial audience with no adjustment for CFA-level prior knowledge. |

**Examples of correct calibration:**
- Do: "Unlike a public equity portfolio where NAV is marked daily, a PE fund NAV depends on GP-determined interim valuations — the implications for return measurement are significant."
- Do not: "Net present value, or NPV, is the discounted value of future cash flows..." *(A CFA charterholder does not need this.)*
- Do: "The preferred return functions similarly to a hurdle rate in public PE/HF incentive structures, but the waterfall mechanics that determine when the GP catches up are unique to private funds."

**Minimum:** Standard ≥ 4 · Dense Technical ≥ 4 · Capstone ≥ 4

## Common Failure Patterns

These are recurring content defects that cause a lesson to score low on multiple dimensions simultaneously. Reviewers should flag any of these by name when present.

---

**Failure 1 · The Wikipedia Problem**
A lesson accurately defines a concept but provides no mechanism, no worked example, and no application to a real decision. Reads like an encyclopedia entry.
*Affects: Explanatory Depth (1–2), Manager-Meeting Transfer (1–2), Judgment-Building Value (1–2)*
*Fix: Add at minimum one worked mechanism and one scenario-based application.*

---

**Failure 2 · The Toy-Number Problem**
Examples use economically implausible figures — a $100 fund, a 2x return on a $50 investment — that produce correct logic but wrong intuition about PE scale, economics, and real-world variation.
*Affects: Realism and Richness of Examples (1–2), Allocator / OCIO Transfer (1–2)*
*Fix: Calibrate to real fund sizes ($300M–$5B buyout range), real return distributions, realistic time horizons. See calibration anchors in Dimension 5.*

---

**Failure 3 · The False-Fluency Problem**
A lesson explains IRR, leverage, or NAV in terms that transfer correctly from public markets — the learner now believes they understand the PE version, but the PE-specific mechanics (IRR timing sensitivity, subscription line distortion, interim NAV dependence on GP policy) are materially different and will mislead.
*Affects: Conceptual Clarity (2–3), Manager-Meeting Transfer (2)*
*Fix: Explicitly mark where the public-markets analogy ends. A single sentence of "here is where this diverges from what you already know" is often sufficient.*

---

**Failure 4 · The Textbook Problem**
A lesson is correct and thorough but has no practitioner voice. No "in a real manager meeting you will hear...", no "LPs often push back on...", no "managers sometimes argue X, but the more rigorous framing is Y." The lesson is classroom-grade, not practitioner-grade.
*Affects: Manager-Meeting Transfer (1–2), Judgment-Building Value (1–2)*
*Fix: Add at least one practitioner framing, one diligence angle, or one example of how this concept is discussed (or obscured) in real manager conversations.*

---

**Failure 5 · The Quiz-That-Tests-Nothing Problem**
Quiz questions that can be answered correctly by re-reading one sentence from the preceding block. Distractors are obviously wrong. Short-response prompt is a paraphrase exercise ("Explain in your own words what IRR means").
*Affects: Retention / Retrieval Support (1–2)*
*Fix: Write distractors that reflect genuine misconceptions a half-informed learner would hold. Write short-response prompts that require synthesis across multiple lesson concepts or application to a novel scenario.*

---

**Failure 6 · The Condescension Problem**
A lesson reteaches WACC, DCF mechanics, general financial accounting, or basic compounding to a CFA charterholder. Time is wasted; the learner's trust in the calibration of the entire curriculum is damaged.
*Affects: Appropriateness for CFA Learner (1–2), Honest Duration Credibility (2–3)*
*Fix: State the public-markets baseline in one sentence, then build immediately from it. "You already know WACC discounting — here is what makes PE hurdle rate mechanics different and why the waterfall matters in a way that a simple discount rate doesn't capture."*

---

**Failure 7 · The Phantom-Duration Problem**
A lesson claims 15 minutes but contains 4,000 words, two complex worked examples, and a multi-part quiz. Or a lesson claims 30 minutes but a CFA reader completes it comfortably in 10. Chronic duration misrepresentation damages the learner's ability to plan sessions and reduces trust in all stated estimates.
*Affects: Honest Duration Credibility (1–2)*
*Fix: Word-count calibration before publication. Use the baseline in Dimension 10: ~240 words/minute active reading, +2–3 minutes per visual, +3–5 minutes for exercise and quiz engagement.*

---

## Review Protocol

Use the following workflow before publishing any lesson:

1. **Score each dimension** on the 1–5 integer scale, with one sentence of justification per score.
2. **Check minimums** against the lesson type using the [Minimum Score Summary](#minimum-score-summary) table. Any dimension below the minimum for its lesson type requires a targeted revision pass before the lesson proceeds.
3. **Scan for failure patterns.** Flag any of the seven named failures above if present.
4. **Compute total score.** Lessons below the minimum sum for their type do not publish.
5. **Sign off** when all dimension scores meet or exceed the minimum for the lesson type.

Premium targets: 44+ / 55 for Standard · 48+ / 55 for Dense Technical · 50+ / 55 for Capstone.

## Glossary

**CFA baseline:** The assumed knowledge floor for this app: DCF/NPV/WACC, CAPM, factor models (Fama-French, Carhart), financial accounting and FSA, fixed income mechanics (duration, convexity, spread analysis), derivatives pricing concepts, portfolio theory (mean-variance, efficient frontier), and the analytical rigor of CFA curriculum-level quantitative thinking. Lessons should never reteach this material; they should build from it.

**False fluency:** A state in which the learner believes they understand a concept but holds a mental model that would mislead them in a real conversation or decision. Most commonly occurs when public-markets analogies are applied to PE concepts without flagging the break (e.g., treating PE IRR as equivalent to an annualized equity return, or treating interim NAV as a marked-to-market valuation).

**Load-bearing visual:** A visual that teaches something the surrounding prose cannot do as efficiently or clearly. Removing it would meaningfully reduce comprehension of the concept. The opposite — a decorative visual — is one that could be removed without any comprehension loss.

**Manager-meeting transfer:** The degree to which lesson content would improve a learner's ability to follow, interrogate, or evaluate a PE manager's presentation, conversation, or written materials. The practical test: if the learner sat in a manager meeting tomorrow, would they do it better because of this lesson?

**Allocator / OCIO transfer:** The degree to which lesson content would improve a learner's ability to make or recommend a portfolio-level PE decision — pacing model inputs, vintage diversification, commitment sizing, manager selection criteria, or ongoing oversight of a PE book. The practical test: would this lesson change what the learner recommends in a portfolio review?

**PME (Public Market Equivalent):** A performance benchmarking methodology that replicates PE cashflow timing in a public index to produce a like-for-like return comparison. Methods include KS-PME (Kaplan-Schoar), mPME (Cambridge Associates), and Direct Alpha. Understanding PME differences is relevant for manager evaluation and benchmark selection.
